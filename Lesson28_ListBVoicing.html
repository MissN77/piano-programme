List B: Expressive Voicing 🎶

You know your List B piece — now let's make it truly beautiful. Voicing means controlling the quality of each note: how it starts, how it sings, how it ends.

🎯 Today you'll learn:

✅ How to control touch for a singing tone
✅ Shaping phrases with weight and breath
✅ Self-assess your List B voicing
💡 The difference between a good performance and a great one is often tone quality. Voicing is the skill that makes music feel alive.

What is Voicing? 🎵

Voicing means choosing how each note sounds — not just which note to play. A heavy finger gives a bright, strong tone. A light, relaxed finger gives a warm, gentle tone.

🎼 Three ways to change your tone:
• Arm weight — let gravity help your fingers sink into the keys gently
• Finger curve — curved fingers give clarity; flatter fingers give warmth
• Key speed — press slowly for soft and smooth; faster for accent
🎵 Voicing in List B:
• The top note (melody) should always be the loudest
• Inner notes should be softer — they support, not compete
• Let the last note of a phrase fade gently — like a breath out
💡 ASD tip: Try pressing a key very slowly — so slowly you can feel it moving. That slow pressure gives the warmest, most beautiful piano tone. Practise this on any single key first.

Voicing Checklist 🎬

Play your List B piece now with full attention to tone. Tick each one you achieve:

  • My tone sounds warm and singing — not harsh or percussive
  • The melody notes are louder than any accompaniment notes
  • My phrases swell and fade — I'm not the same volume all the way through
  • The final note of each phrase fades gently, like a breath out
  • My arm feels relaxed — no tension in my shoulder or wrist
  • I could imagine a singer performing this melody
  • It sounds genuinely beautiful — I'm proud of this phrase

Practice Tips 💡

🎯 This week, practise:
1. Play your List B piece with your eyes closed — focus entirely on how it sounds
2. Record yourself and listen: does it sound warm or harsh?
3. Practise the first phrase 10 times, each time trying to make the tone slightly more beautiful
4. Ask yourself after each phrase: "Would that make someone feel something?"
💡 ASD tip:
If "expressive" feels vague, try giving each phrase a specific feeling: "this phrase is calm", "this one is hopeful", "this one is sad". Your fingers will naturally change their touch to match the feeling you've decided on.
