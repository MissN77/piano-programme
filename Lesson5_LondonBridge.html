🎉 Today we learn London Bridge!

This week's big idea: not all notes are the same length! Some notes last 1 beat and some last 2 beats. Learning this is a huge step in reading music.

🎵 What you'll learn today:
  • Quarter note (♩) = 1 beat
  • Half note (𝅗𝅥) = 2 beats
  • Whole note (𝅝) = 4 beats
  • Quarter rest (𝄽) = 1 beat of silence
  • Playing London Bridge with the right hand
💡 ASD Support

We'll clap the rhythm first before touching the keys. This separates the challenge of counting from the challenge of finding notes — much easier!

🎹 Find the notes on your keyboard

London Bridge uses 6 notes: C · D · E · F · G · A. Your hand starts in C position (RH, finger 1 on C). To reach A, slide your hand up slightly so finger 5 lands on A.

🥁 Note values — counting beats

Before you play, let's understand how long each note lasts. Music is like a clock — the beat is the steady tick. Different note shapes tell you how many ticks to hold each note.

♩
Quarter note
1 beat · "Ta"
𝅗𝅥
Half note
2 beats · "Ta-aa"
𝅝
Whole note
4 beats · "Ta-aa-aa-aa"
🎵 Try this! Clap and count aloud: "1, 2, 3, 4 / 1, 2, 3, 4" — keep it steady like a clock. When you see a half note, hold the clap for 2 counts.
💡 Visual Rhythm Cards

Use a visual timer (like a Time Timer) to show how long each note lasts. Quarter = ¼ of the dial, half = ½. This makes abstract counting concrete and visual!

✅ Practice checklist

  • Clapped the full rhythm counting aloud (3 times)
  • Found all 6 notes on the keyboard (C D E F G A)
  • Played the melody slowly at ♩=60
  • Said the beat numbers aloud while playing
  • Tried at ♩=80

🏠 This week at home

  • Play London Bridge 3 times each day
  • Each time, count the beats aloud ("1, 1, 1, 1, 1, 1, 1-2 …")
  • Can you clap the rhythm without looking at the screen?
  • Try tapping the rhythm on your knee first, then play on keys
💡 ASD Support — Separate the challenges

If both counting AND finding notes feels hard at the same time, do them separately: first clap just the rhythm (no keyboard), then play just the notes (no counting). Then put them together.
