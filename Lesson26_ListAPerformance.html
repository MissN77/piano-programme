💡 Examiners don't just want correct notes — they want musical playing. Phrasing and dynamics are what separate a pass from a distinction!

What is Phrasing? 🌊

A phrase is like a musical sentence — it has a beginning, a middle, and an end. Just as your voice rises and falls when you speak, music should breathe and shape itself.

🎵 How to shape a phrase:
• Rise — as the melody goes up, grow a little louder
• Peak — the highest note is often the most important
• Fall — as the melody comes back down, soften slightly
• Breathe — tiny lift at the end of each phrase before the next
🎼 In List A pieces:
• Phrases are usually 2 or 4 bars long
• The first phrase often asks a musical "question"
• The second phrase gives the "answer"
• This is called question and answer phrasing!
💡 ASD tip: Try humming your piece first. Where does your voice naturally rise and fall? That's your phrasing guide — let your fingers do what your voice does.

Performance Checklist 🎬

Play your List A piece now. After each run-through, tick the boxes you achieved:

  • I shaped my phrases — melody rose and fell naturally
  • I used dynamics — I wasn't the same volume all the way through
  • My staccato notes were short and crisp (not heavy)
  • My legato notes were smooth and connected
  • I played confidently — my List A had energy and character
  • I kept going through any mistakes without stopping
  • It sounded like a march or a dance — not just notes!

Practice Tips 💡

🎯 This week, practise:
1. Play your List A piece three times in a row — once for notes, once for dynamics, once for phrasing
2. Record yourself (phone is fine) and listen back — does it sound musical?
3. Practise the loudest bar and the softest bar separately
4. Count yourself in before you play — feel the pulse before the first note
💡 ASD tip:
Recording yourself can feel uncomfortable, but it is one of the most powerful ways to improve. You don't have to listen to it with anyone else — it's just for you. Even 10 seconds of recording can show you something useful.
