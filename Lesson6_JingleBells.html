🎵 Welcome to Lesson 6!

Today you'll play the opening of Jingle Bells — one of the most famous melodies in the world! You already know all the notes you need. This lesson introduces 4/4 time signatures and bar lines — the rules that keep music organised and predictable.

🧠 ASD Note for Parent/Carer

Music has rules, just like maths — this can be very appealing for many children with ASD. Bar lines divide music into equal chunks so you always know where you are. Try saying: "4/4 means every bar has exactly 4 beats — no more, no less." You can also draw bar lines in different colours to make them visually distinct.

🎯 Lesson Goal

Play the opening 4 bars of Jingle Bells (right hand only) at a steady tempo, counting beats aloud. Understand what a time signature means.

📋 What You'll Learn

  • What a time signature means (top and bottom numbers)
  • How bar lines divide music into equal groups
  • What a double bar line and repeat sign look like
  • Play a 4-bar melody using E, G, C, D (right hand)

⏱️ Suggested Timing

  • Warm-up: 5 minutes (scales/finger exercises)
  • Theory (time signature): 10 minutes
  • Learning the melody: 15 minutes
  • Free play / fun: 5 minutes

📐 What Is a Time Signature?

At the start of every piece of music, you'll see two numbers stacked on top of each other — this is the time signature. It tells you the rules for counting beats.

4
4

Top number (4): There are 4 beats in every bar


Bottom number (4): Each beat is worth a quarter note (crotchet)

🧠 ASD Note

The time signature is like a rule that never changes throughout the piece. Every single bar will have exactly 4 beats. This is predictable and consistent — once you know the rule, you can always count correctly.

📊 4 Beats in a Bar

In 4/4 time, beat 1 is the strong beat (louder). Beats 2, 3, and 4 are weaker. This creates a natural rhythm: STRONG – weak – weak – weak.

Each group of 4 = one bar. Bar lines divide them:

💡 How Bar Lines Work

A bar line is a vertical line on the staff that separates one bar from the next. A double bar line (two lines) marks the end of a section. A repeat sign (double bar + dots) means "go back and play this section again."

🎙️ Counting Practice

Before playing, practise saying the counts aloud. For Jingle Bells, it goes:

Bar 1: E — E — E — — (count: 1 – 2 – 3–4)
Bar 2: E — E — E — — (count: 1 – 2 – 3–4)
Bar 3: E — G — C — D (count: 1 – 2 – 3 – 4)
Bar 4: E — — — — — — — (count: 1–2–3–4, hold)

🧠 ASD Tip — Counting Aloud

Count every beat aloud before playing: "1, 2, 3, 4 / 1, 2, 3, 4 / ..." Some children find it helpful to tap the beat on their knee with one hand while the other hand plays. This builds physical pattern recognition — an area many children with ASD excel in.

✅ Today's Practice Checklist

🏠 Daily Practice Plan (15 min)

  • Days 1–2: Practise bar 1 and bar 2 separately. Count "1-2-3-4" aloud. E–E–E(hold 2).
  • Days 3–4: Add bar 3 (E–G–C–D). Play bars 1–3 in sequence.
  • Day 5: Play all 4 bars. Hold the final E for 4 full beats.
  • Days 6–7: Play all 4 bars from memory at ♩=60, then try ♩=80.
💡 Theory Activity

Draw a blank staff and write 4 bars with bar lines. Fill in the notes from today using note heads. Count the beats in each bar — do they all add up to 4? This visual exercise reinforces the concept of bar lines as "equal containers."
